**Project:** Clinical Note Assistant — Pre-Stanmore AI for Biomedical Engineering  
**Repository:** github.com/arapkirui513-hub/clinical-note-assistant

## 1. Why Safety Disclaimers Are Not Optional

The clinical note assistant uses `gpt-4o-mini` to summarise free-text medical notes into structured JSON fields: `chief_complaint`, `diagnosis`, and `plan`. This is a **natural language processing task applied to clinical content**, which means its outputs could, in principle, influence how a clinician understands a patient's presentation.

Even a tool clearly labelled as experimental — used informally, in a moment of time pressure, by a clinician who "just wants a quick summary" — could cause patient harm if it produces a plausible-sounding but incorrect output and that output goes unchecked.

The W5D5 validation findings make this concrete: on 10 MTSamples notes, the model produced four distinct failure patterns, all of which would be clinically meaningful if acted upon:

| Failure Pattern | Potential Clinical Consequence |
|---|---|
| Pre-op diagnosis copied verbatim without checking operative findings | Incorrect post-operative care plan; wrong diagnosis recorded |
| Quantitative/objective data dropped (weight loss, Coumadin, pacemaker) | Missed medication interaction; surgical risk overlooked |
| Vague diagnosis when specificity available | Ambiguous handover; delayed escalation |
| Reasoning field does not control output | No reliability guarantee from CoT; outputs appear confident when they should not |

These are not edge cases. They appeared in a sample of 10 notes. The disclaimer and per-output caveat exist to make the tool's fundamental unreliability legible to anyone who encounters it.

## 2. Component 1: The Disclaimer Banner

### What it does

A persistent banner is displayed at the top of the Streamlit app on every load and every page state. It reads:

> ⚠️ **NOT FOR CLINICAL USE.** This tool is a prototype for educational purposes only. Outputs are generated by an AI model and may be incorrect, incomplete, or misleading. Do not use to inform clinical decisions.

### Design rationale

**Persistent placement.** The banner is rendered before the input widget, not after. This ensures it is visible before the user submits a note, not only after they have already read the output. A disclaimer placed below results is less likely to be read and easier to discount after the fact.

**Plain language, not legal boilerplate.** The text avoids terms like "without warranty" or "limitation of liability." The target reader in a real-world scenario is a clinician in a hurry, not a lawyer. The banner states plainly what the tool is (a prototype), what it cannot do (inform clinical decisions), and what the output is (AI-generated, potentially wrong).

**Explicit scope.** "Educational purposes only" is specific. It signals that the appropriate use case is learning about LLM behaviour and prompt engineering — not workflow support or documentation assistance.

**Visual differentiation.** The banner uses a warning colour (amber/yellow in the dark theme, or Streamlit's `st.warning` component) to distinguish it from neutral informational text. Users are trained by convention to treat amber/yellow banners as requiring attention before proceeding.

### What it is not

The banner is not a substitute for governance. It does not make the tool safe for clinical use. It is a communication layer that makes the tool's limitations explicit — so that the demonstrator (in this case, a portfolio reviewer or course instructor) can see that the developer understands those limitations.

## 3. Component 2: The Per-Output Caveat

### What it does

Every structured output block is appended with a caveat rendered below the `diagnosis` and `plan` fields:

> ⚠️ AI-generated summary. May contain errors, omissions, or hallucinations. Verify against the original note before use.

### Design rationale

**Point-of-use placement.** The per-output caveat appears at the moment the user is most likely to act on the information — immediately after reading the summary. Research on warning compliance consistently shows that warnings placed at the point of decision are more effective than those placed before or after the decision moment. The banner addresses the session-level risk; the per-output caveat addresses the output-level risk.

**Contextually accurate language.** The caveat names specific failure modes: errors, omissions, and hallucinations. This is more informative than a generic "AI may make mistakes" notice because it tells the user *what kinds* of mistakes to check for. All three appeared in the W5D5 validation:

- **Errors:** incorrect diagnosis (Pattern 3 — vague or wrong)
- **Omissions:** dropped clinical data (Pattern 2 — weight loss, Coumadin, pacemaker)
- **Hallucinations:** operative note Pattern 1 — pre-op diagnosis reproduced as if it were the surgical finding, without basis in the operative text

**Actionable instruction.** "Verify against the original note before use" gives the user a specific action, not just a general warning. It anchors the summary as a starting point for reading the note, not a replacement for it.

**Repeated on every output.** The caveat appears every time a note is summarised, not just on the first run. Familiarity effects (habituation, "warning fatigue") are a real risk with persistent banners — users stop reading them after a few exposures. Attaching the caveat to each output ensures it remains contextually anchored rather than becoming background noise.

## 4. Relationship to Validation Findings

The specific wording of both components was informed by the W5D5 validation errors, not written generically.

The operative-report failure (Pattern 1 — fixed in W6D1 via few-shot example) motivated the phrase "verify against the original note": the model had produced a plausible summary that was factually wrong relative to the source document. The fix in `app.py` (adding an operative-report few-shot example) reduces the frequency of this error, but does not eliminate it. The caveat remains necessary because few-shot prompting is a probabilistic intervention, not a guarantee.

The quantitative-data failure (Pattern 2) motivated explicitly naming "omissions" in the caveat. A user who knows to check for omissions is more likely to notice that a pacemaker or anticoagulant has disappeared from the summary than one who only knows the output "might be wrong."

The CoT finding (Pattern 4 — reasoning field does not control output) motivated the decision not to display the `reasoning` field in the app UI. Displaying it would imply the model's explanation of its reasoning is reliable, when validation showed it is not. The per-output caveat reinforces this: the output should be checked against source, not treated as self-validating because a reasoning chain was produced.

## 5. What These Measures Do Not Address

These two safety components are appropriate for a **portfolio prototype**. They are insufficient for any real clinical deployment. The following risks remain unaddressed:

**No identity or access control.** Any user who runs the app can submit any note. There is no authentication, audit log, or record of who submitted what.

**No output logging.** Errors are not recorded. There is no mechanism for systematic post-hoc review of failure cases, which is a prerequisite for quality assurance in deployed clinical software.

**No clinician-in-the-loop enforcement.** The disclaimers request that users verify outputs, but nothing in the system enforces this. A deployed tool would need to require clinician sign-off before a summary could be saved or forwarded.

**No regulatory classification or conformity assessment.** Software that supports clinical disease support clinical decision-making falls under the UK Medical Devices Regulations 2002 (as amended) and the MHRA's evolving AI as a Medical Device framework. A real deployment would require a conformity assessment, a technical file, and (for Class IIa) involvement of a notified body. Disclaimers do not substitute for regulatory compliance.

These limitations are documented here to demonstrate awareness, not to suggest the tool is close to deployable. The prototype is scoped for educational use only.

## 6. Summary

| Component | Placement | Primary Risk Addressed | Validation Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Disclaimer banner | Top of app, persistent | Session-level: user does not know tool is unsafe | General; all 4 failure patterns |
| Per-output caveat | Below each structured output | Output-level: user acts on a specific incorrect summary | Patterns 1 (hallucination), 2 (omission), 3 (vague diagnosis) |

Both components are necessary. Neither is sufficient on its own, and neither makes the tool safe for clinical use. They make the tool's limitations legible — which is the appropriate goal for a prototype at this stage.
